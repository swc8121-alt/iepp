## 1. Introduction

IEPP v0.4 introduced identity defined as continuity of trajectory.

IEPP v0.5 extends this concept into a policy layer.

If identity is defined as continuity,
systems must determine how continuity is verified,
shared,
challenged,
and governed.

IEPP v0.5 explores operational structures enabling continuity-based identity.

The goal is not rigid control,
but coherent coordination between independent verifiers.

---

## 2. Motivation for policy layer

Trajectory continuity alone is not sufficient.

Identity systems require operational context:

when verification occurs  
how verification confidence is determined  
who maintains canonical lineage  
how forks are evaluated  
how disputes are resolved  

Policy layer provides coordination logic for identity evaluation.

Without policy layer,
continuity signals remain isolated observations.

With policy layer,
continuity signals become interoperable identity infrastructure.

## 3. Multi-verifier authentication

Single verification event may be insufficient in adversarial environments.

IEPP allows multiple independent verification events.

Examples:

parallel verification requests

sequential verification requests

periodic verification intervals

event-triggered verification

Multiple verifications increase confidence of continuity.

Independent verification contexts increase robustness.

Multi-verifier structure reduces reliance on single trust authority.

## 4. Continuous authentication

Identity may be evaluated continuously rather than once.

Possible modes:

adaptive verification frequency

Continuous authentication aligns with trajectory-based identity concept.

Identity becomes observable over time.
